No, it's not just you. The Philippines is indeed getting more humid.

This heatmap shows the maximum heat index recorded in all Pagasa stations from April 15 to 30. A heat index measures the "real feel" of the weather to the body.

In two weeks, Baguio City, north of the Philippines, recorded a peak heat index above 32 degrees Celsius, an unusually warm weather in the coldest part of the country

Beyond Baguio, the situation was worse. Residents in majority of areas were either told to exercise "extreme caution"...

…or warned of potential "danger" from extreme heat on one's health such as heat cramps or stroke.

A prevailing El Nino phenomenon since last year is contributing to the scintillating heat, but this year's scorching summer is unlikely to be an aberration. For the past decade ending 2023, temperatures in the country are rising year after year, data from the weather bureau show.

That, in turn, is making warm weather more common throughout the day.

It's more hot in the Philippines

The country suffers a scorching dry season this year. Data show temperatures have been rising for at least the past decade.

By Prinz Magtulis


In Science Garden in Quezon City and the Nino Aquino International Airport in Pasay, average temperatures this dry season— which runs from March to May or June— are already trending their highest since at least 2021, data from Weather Manila, a non-government weather research institute, showed.
Pagasa's own data show a bleaker picture. Over the past decade, even minimum temperatures in a day are slowly rising. These readings are typical at night when temperatures naturally go down as the sun sets. That they are increasing over time means that reprieve from heat during nighttime has become less pronounced.

RECORD-BREAKING.Chart from Weather Manila showing daily average temperatures in Science Garden in Quezon City and Ninoy Aquino International Airport (NAIA) from March 1 to May 31 for current and previous years.
Source: Pagasa

But time of the day alone does not dictate the level of heat people experience, your location does, too. While minimum monthly temperatures have indeed increased broadly, the pace and scale of rise differ. For instance, March and April appear to be the hottest months since 2014, but the uptick in temperatures in Science Garden far outpace that in Mactan.
In fact, in areas like Basco in Batanes, Zamboanga and Daet in Catanduanes, minimum temperatures during the dry season more likely even declined from their 2014 levels, indicating that these areas have gotten cooler. Meanwhile, Clark City in Pampanga has seen minimum temperatures during months of March to May pick up.
Gerry Bagtasa, professor at the Institute of Environmental Science and Meteorology at the University of the Philippines-Diliman, attributed these temperature differences to the varying levels of urbanization in these areas.
"In urban areas, land use/cover is mostly built up, as opposed to mostly vegetation in rural. Ground mainly made up of concrete with little vegetation (little evaporation) tends to make urban hold heat more…and longer, resulting in higher nighttime temps," said Bagtasa, also the founder of Weather Manila.

A 2021 study published on the International Journal of Climatology found that urbanization in the Philippines has contributed to the warming of temperatures due to the "urban heat island effect", where increased use of concrete and higher heat emissions from businesses and transportation result into longer retention of heat in cities.
One such city is Baguio, where a Pagasa weather station is located. While the city, dubbed the summer capital of the Philippines, traditionally has a much colder climate than the rest of the archipelago because of its high altitude, the place is no longer as cold it used to be.
On a yearly basis, Baguio's average minimum temperature rose 1.5 degrees Celsius from 2014 to 2023, Pagasa data showed. Temperatures in the city, situated five hours north of Manila, drop between December and February and last year fell as much as 14.4 degrees Celsius. Just a decade ago, minimum monthly temperatures plummet as much as 10 degrees Celsius, and up to about 12 degrees five years after that.

While temperatures are already beating records this year, Pagasa said the worst is yet to come and that intense heat are likely to persist until mid-May. On the ground, measures are already being taken to cushion the heat's impact on public health such remote working options for employees and virtual learning for students after the government suspended in-person instruction. The Supreme Court also ordered judges to check on the impact of heat on inmates in typically congested jails.

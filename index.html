No, it's not just you. The Philippines is indeed getting hotter.

For the past decade ending 2023, average minimum temperature in the country has increased, in some cases by a full percentage point, data from the weather bureau show.

Warmer weather was monitored not only during El Nino years like 2014-2016 and 2023 when warm air in the Pacific typically intensified the scorching heat in the region.

But traditionally cooler months of December and January had also seen minimum temperatures go up. Even during the day, nights are becoming hotter.

This year is no likely to be different.

It's more hot in the Philippines

Filipinos used to warm weather are finding this year's dry season tougher to bear

By Prinz Magtulis

May 3, 2024


The Philippines' tropical climate has long been one of its tourism selling points. Tourists from colder parts of the world flock to the country's pristine beaches during dry months of March to May.
But for Filipinos, enduring its so-called “summer” this year has been tougher: temperatures have peaked and elevated humidity has made heat remarkably intense even for residents used to warm weather all year round. Pagasa, the state weather bureau, has warned the worst is yet to come.
This year, Pagasa has consistently reported rising heat index across its 56 monitoring stations nationwide. Maximum temperatures during the day are indeed going up, and in some cases breaking new records. But equally important is the other end of the scale, which is also showing higher readings for minimum temperatures of the day, typically recorded at night.

"Nighttime temperatures are increasing," said Gerry Bagtasa, professor at the Institute of Environmental Science and Meteorology at the University of the Philippines-Diliman.
At the Ninoy Aquino International Airport, where Pagasa has a reading station, average minimum temperature rose to 25.7 degrees Celsius last year from just 25 degrees Celsius in 2014. Temperatures picked up faster up north in Laoag in Ilocos Norte, where minimum readings rose 1.2 degrees to 24.1 degrees Celsius.
Across Pagasa's stations, more developed cities like Davao and Mactan are experiencing higher temperatures than least developed ones like Basco in Batanes, General Santos City and Tuguegarao in Cagayan. While climate change can be an easy scapegoat on this warming phenomenon, Bagtasa said it's much more than that.
"This usually points to urbanization," he said. "In urban areas, land use/cover is mostly built up, as opposed to mostly vegetation in rural. Ground mainly made up of concrete with little vegetation (little evaporation) tends to make urban hold heat more…and longer, resulting in higher nighttime temps."
Even Baguio City was not spared. While the summer capital of the Philippines still experiences much lower temperatures than the rest of the archipelago, the place is no longer as cold as it was before.

However, it is difficult to draw conclusions from these numbers, which also do not reflect efficiency in disposing of cases. Case backlogs are an enduring problem in the broader judiciary, and the Supreme Court has made efforts to speed up the resolution of cases, both within its own backyard and in lower courts where it exercises administrative powers.
By tenure, the longest sitting justice to date is Senior Associate Justice Marvic Leonen, who was appointed by the late president Benigno Aquino III, in November 2012. That makes Leonen the ponente of over 800 decisions in court for the past decade, the most among sitting justices, according to court data.
Controlling for the number of months they have occupied their chambers so far, Leonen places third behind Associate Justices Henri Jean Paul Inting and Ramon Paul Hernando on the average number of penned decisions promulgated every month, our analysis showed.
On average, the justices wrote about four decisions a month, data showed.
